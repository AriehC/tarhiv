There is no precedent in human history for the revival of the Hebrew language. Many ancient languages have died — Latin, Sumerian, Akkadian — but none of them has ever come back to life as a mother tongue. Hebrew is the exception: a language that remained for approximately 1,700 years solely a sacred tongue, read and written but not spoken in daily life — and within a single generation, it was transformed into the vibrant living language of a modern state.

## From Sacred Language to Street Language

The most dramatic transformation in Hebrew's history occurred within a single generation. In 1881, Eliezer Ben-Yehuda arrived in the Land of Israel with a dream that seemed insane: to turn Hebrew — a language that had not been spoken in everyday life for nearly 2,000 years — into children's mother tongue. His son, Ben-Zion Ben-Yehuda (Itamar Ben-Avi), was the first child in modern times whose mother tongue was Hebrew.

He refused to allow his wife and son to speak any language other than Hebrew — even when there were no Hebrew words for basic concepts like "doll," "ice cream," or "newspaper." When a word didn't exist, he invented it. He worked on an enormous dictionary — "The Complete Dictionary of Ancient and Modern Hebrew" — written throughout his entire life and completed only after his death, spanning 17 volumes.

## The Challenge: Inventing a Modern Language

When Ben-Yehuda began his endeavor, Hebrew had no words for modern concepts. There was no word for "newspaper," "fashion," "ice cream," or "electricity." An entire language had to be created virtually from scratch — while preserving ancient Hebrew roots.

## Constant Decline: 1.2 Meters Per Year

The most alarming statistic about the Dead Sea is its rate of water level decline. Since the 1960s, the Dead Sea's water level has been dropping at an average of approximately 1.2 meters per year — a total decline of more than 35 meters. The primary causes are the diversion of Jordan River waters for agriculture and drinking, and water pumping by Dead Sea mineral extraction plants for potash production.

<InfoCard type="warning" title="Sinkholes: A Growing Danger">
One of the most dramatic consequences of the declining water level is the formation of sinkholes. Since the 1980s, over 6,000 sinkholes have appeared along the Dead Sea's shores. They form when fresh groundwater dissolves underground salt layers, creating cavities that cause the surface to collapse. These sinkholes have caused severe damage to infrastructure, roads, and tourism facilities, and have even forced the evacuation of entire kibbutzim.
</InfoCard>

## Chemical Composition: Not Just Salty Water

The Dead Sea is fundamentally different from any other body of water. While regular ocean water contains about 3.5% salt (primarily sodium chloride), the Dead Sea contains 34.2% minerals — nearly ten times as much. But the difference isn't just in quantity — it's in composition.

## Timeline: Geological History

The Dead Sea was formed as a result of tectonic processes that began millions of years ago. The Syrian-African Rift — one of the largest geological fractures in the world — created the Jordan Valley and the depression in which the Dead Sea sits.

<InfoCard type="info" title="The Red-Dead Canal: Dream or Reality?">
The most ambitious project is the **Red-Dead Canal** — a conduit that would transport water from the Red Sea to the Dead Sea. The plan includes building a desalination facility along the way, to provide drinking water to Jordan, Israel, and the Palestinian Authority. However, the project raises environmental concerns: altering the Dead Sea's chemical composition could cause the water to whiten and damage the area's unique natural phenomena.
</InfoCard>
